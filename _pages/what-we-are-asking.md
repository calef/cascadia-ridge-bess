---
title: "What We're Asking"
---

We support grid storage. PSE needs it for CETA compliance. Climate change is real, the grid has to decarbonize, and battery storage is part of the answer. We understand why this site was chosen — the Mt. Si substation is adjacent, the Eastside load center is nearby, and undeveloped land next to transmission infrastructure is hard to find.

We're not trying to block the project. We're asking for two things that would address the core community concerns.

## 1. Sodium-ion or equivalent non-lithium-ion chemistry

Jupiter Power's own $500M Peak Energy contract shows this is where the industry is going. Sodium-ion eliminates:

- Thermal runaway (the mechanism behind every major BESS fire)
- Hydrogen fluoride and heavy metal emissions
- 24/7 cooling fan noise (passive cooling, no mechanical noise)

The facility won't operate until late 2028. Sodium-ion will be commercially available before then.

## 2. Tree retention on the west (Parkway-facing) side

Critical area buffers protect the north and east edges of the site. But the west side — the side facing Snoqualmie Parkway, the side the community actually sees — has no protection. Keeping the existing tree line costs the project nothing and preserves the visual buffer between the facility and the neighborhood.

## What we've proposed to King County

We've identified two amendments to King County's BESS ordinance (Ordinance 19824) that would address these issues county-wide:

1. **Expand the Condition 29 alternatives analysis to include battery chemistry.** If a safer, commercially available chemistry exists, the applicant should have to explain why they're not using it. This doesn't ban any technology — it requires transparency.

2. **Scale financial responsibility to facility size and siting context.** The current ordinance sets a flat $1 million liability threshold regardless of whether a facility is 2 MWh or 520 MWh, and regardless of proximity to homes or sensitive habitat. The proposed amendment ties liability to capacity with adjustments for residential proximity and critical areas.

Both proposals build on mechanisms already in the ordinance.
